You know how this journey starts

You take a ten years old, who doesn't go well with other kids but has a knack for mathematics, you give him an 8 bit computer and a book, and a few years later he's graduating in IT from a university or two.

A bit earlier, the boy gets a job as a systems analyst, but it's boring in the long run, so he moves to programming. He finds a mentor to learn from, and to point to the right books. Few years more, and he is a team leader.

When the boy is versatile enough to handle all the parts of the development process, and communicative enough to understand and solve business problems, you finally have a software developer.

And where it goes

That's pretty much how it worked for me. People are defined by their quirks and traits, and I've soon discovered, I cannot work with bad code. It just steals all the fun and motivation I have. This pushed me into Test Driven Development and Software Craftsmanship movement. I just didn't have any other choice.

The larger the system gets, the harder it is to get it right. After failing miserably, I've finally found, that Domain Driven Design provides a good foundation for handling that problem. Together with processes following Agile Manifesto, like Scrum or Kanban, I was finally able to deliver quality on time. And it didn't turn into a big ball of mud with increasing size.

Oh, and by the way, I am Jakub Nabrdalik. Nice to meet you.

Most of my professional life, I worked in software houses: zeto, smi, k2, TouK (an extremely agile software house with very wide area of expertise) writing thin client dedicated applications for external customers, in languages like Groovy, Java, C#, PHP, and using plethora of technologies. I do everything from analysis, programming, modeling, architecture, coaching to managing projects.

Scott Adams once wrote, that the biggest correlation you can find between all the winning candidates for president of the USA, is height and lots of hair, which I suppose explains, why I am usually a team leader or a project manager. Lately, I've joined 4finance and got promoted into the Head of Software Development in Poland role, where my job is to organize everything (including architecture) so that other people can do all the cool hacking. My focus is still on programming. I just love writing code and solving business problems.

Some of the systems, I've worked on, include:

Finance

Loan management, anti-fraud and lending platform solutions are interesting domains themselves, but what is even more interesting, is changing the architecture, culture, organization and methods of a relatively small corporation to handle several products in 12 countries, and not get hurt by the Conway's law while growing extremely fast (triple the development size in a year). Microservices, devops culture and self organized Scrum teams come in handy.

Mobile Banking

If USA is the third world in terms of personal financial services and Europe is the second, Africa is the first. We've built a mobile banking system for a bank in Nigeria, months before their competitors managed to integrate an out-of-the-box product.

Were we cheaper? Sure. Were we faster? Definitely. Do we have more features? Absolutely. Were we easier to cooperate with? Hell yeah!

Telco

There are 4 big telcos in Poland: T-Mobile (PTC), P4, Polkomtel, Orange. I've worked on dedicated applications for 3 of them, including network monitoring, invoicing and customer relation management systems.

Telcos are the very definition of enterprise-level and big data. You don't get more enterprise than that, unless you are watching Star Trek. Projects for telcos are always interesting, their requirements are rarely trivial, and the fact that what you do have direct influence on tens of millions of people is fascinating.

eCommerce

Boy, I've done a lot of those. Mostly small to medium size, for companies like Nokia, DaimlerChrysler, Hoopla, Raypath, KDC, and many others. Not really surprising, given you can buy everything on the Internet right now.

This domain is sweet because you tend to use it yourself, thus you are getting to understand not only the technical problems, but also the very core of the business. Doesn't matter how good your software is, if you cannot make the pricing rules flexible enough to get on the top of price comparison engines.

Data management systems

How do you create a system for handling all the data? How about building base blocks, some samples and letting your customer configure the rest? You know, Lego/Ikea style. Suprisingly, it worked.

Content management systems

I've never had so much fun as with content management systems. This is the heart of information technology. I was lucky to be able to build one from the scratch in technologies and times, when not everybody had a CMS around.

Enterprise resource planning

I've had my share here as well. Both with creating ERP from the scratch, and integrating open-source system with everything else around. This domain tends to grow exponentially. Just like requirements.
